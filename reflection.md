# Reflection — Profile Comparisons

## High-Energy Pop vs. Chill Lofi

These two profiles produced completely different results, which is a good sign that the system is actually reading the user's preferences rather than returning the same songs to everyone. The High-Energy Pop listener got upbeat, fast-paced tracks like "Sunrise City" and "Gym Hero" — songs with pounding tempos and bright, happy moods that match what someone might play while working out or getting ready to go out. The Chill Lofi listener, on the other hand, received quiet, low-key tracks like "Library Rain" and "Midnight Coding" — songs that feel slow and relaxed, more suited to studying or winding down. The reason the outputs look so different comes down to three things the system checks: genre label, mood tag, and how energetic the song feels on a scale of zero to one. Because these two profiles sit on opposite ends of all three of those scales, almost no songs overlapped in their top five. This tells us the scoring logic is doing its most basic job — separating very different listeners into very different results.

## Deep Intense Rock vs. Adversarial Conflicting-Preference (high energy, sad mood, soul genre)

This comparison reveals where the system starts to struggle. The Deep Intense Rock profile worked well — "Storm Runner" came out on top because it matched the genre, the mood, and the energy level almost perfectly, and the system had a clear winner to point to. The adversarial profile was designed to be contradictory on purpose: it asked for soul music with a sad mood, but also wanted very high energy (0.95). The problem is that the only soul song in the catalog ("Empty Frames") is slow and quiet, with an energy level of just 0.44 — the exact opposite of what the energy preference asked for. So the system faced a conflict it has no way to resolve: the one song that matches the genre and mood feels nothing like what the energy preference describes. Rather than flagging this contradiction, the system quietly ranked high-energy songs that matched nothing emotionally above the one song that actually fit the mood. This tells us the system treats every rule as equally independent — it cannot recognize when preferences contradict each other, and it will always produce a confident-looking list even when the request doesn't make sense.
